In some areas like Wazirpur, the AQI has skyrocketed to a staggering 477, while PM2.5 levels have reached 335 µg/m³ — that's more than 5 times the government's safe limit of 60 µg/m³.</p><p>To put this in perspective: breathing Delhi's air right now is equivalent to smoking several cigarettes a day. Every breath you take contains microscopic particles that lodge deep in your lungs, potentially causing long-term damage.</p><h3>Understanding the AQI Scale</h3><p>The Air Quality Index is measured on a scale that helps us understand what we're breathing:</p><p>• 0-50: Good — Fresh air, safe for everyone</p><p>• 51-100: Satisfactory — Acceptable air quality</p><p>• 101-200: Moderate — Sensitive groups may experience issues</p><p>• 201-300: Poor — Everyone may feel discomfort</p><p>• 301-400: Very Poor — Respiratory issues common</p><p>• 401-500: Severe — Health emergency, affects everyone</p><p>Delhi is currently <strong>hovering around 400+</strong>, firmly in the <strong>'severe' zone</strong>. The forecast suggests it will remain in the 'very poor' to 'severe' range for the next six days, with no immediate relief in sight.</p><p>Why Is This Happening?</p><p>Delhi's winter air crisis isn't new, but understanding the causes helps us prepare better:</p><p><strong>1. Stubble Burning</strong>: Farmers in neighboring states burn crop residue, sending thick smoke into Delhi's atmosphere.</p><p><strong>2. Vehicular Emissions</strong>: Millions of vehicles on Delhi's roads contribute significantly to PM2.5 and PM10 pollution.</p><p><strong>3. Industrial Pollution</strong>: Factories and thermal power plants in the NCR region add to the toxic mix.</p><p><strong>4. Construction Dust</strong>: Despite regulations, dust from construction sites remains a major contributor.</p><p><strong>5. Weather Conditions</strong>: Low wind speeds and temperature inversions trap pollutants close to the ground, creating the thick smog blanket we see.</p><p><strong>6. Firecracker Usage</strong>: Post-Diwali effects</p><h3>Safety Measures: How to Protect Yourself</h3><p>While we can't control the air quality, we can take steps to minimize its impact on our health. Here's a list of safety measures you can take:</p><p><strong>✓ 1. Wear N95/N99 Masks</strong></p><p>Not all masks are created equal. Regular surgical or cloth masks won't cut it. Invest in N95 or N99 masks that can filter out PM2.5 particles. Wear them whenever you step outside, especially during peak pollution hours (early morning and evening).</p><p><strong>✓ 2. Limit Outdoor Activities</strong></p><p>This is especially crucial for children, elderly people, and those with existing respiratory or heart conditions. Avoid morning walks, outdoor exercise, and playing outside when AQI is above 300. Check the AQI before planning any outdoor activities.</p><p><strong>✓ 3. Use Air Purifiers Indoors</strong></p><p>Invest in a HEPA air purifier for your home, especially for bedrooms. Keep windows and doors closed during high pollution hours. However, remember that purifiers have limitations — they work best in enclosed spaces.</p><p><strong>✓ 4. Stay Hydrated and Eat Right</strong></p><p>Drink plenty of water to help your body flush out toxins. Include antioxidant-rich foods in your diet — green tea, jaggery, turmeric, and fresh fruits like oranges and pomegranates can help combat the effects of pollution.</p><p><strong>✓ 5. Monitor AQI Daily</strong></p><p>Download AQI monitoring apps or check websites like AQI.in, CPCB's Sameer app, or AccuWeather. Plan your day based on pollution levels. If AQI crosses 400, it's a health emergency — stay indoors as much as possible.</p><p><strong>✓ 6. Create a Clean Room</strong></p><p>Designate one room in your house as a "clean room" with an air purifier running continuously. Use this space for sleeping and studying. Seal any cracks in doors and windows.</p><p><strong>✓ 7. Avoid Smoking and Burning Incense</strong></p><p>Indoor air pollution from cigarettes, agarbatti, or burning anything adds to the problem. Keep your indoor air as clean as</p><p><strong>✓ 8. Use Public Transport or Carpool</strong></p><p>Every vehicle on the road adds to pollution. If you must go out, use metro, buses, or carpool. Better yet, work from home if your employer allows it during high pollution days.</p><p><strong>✓ 9. Consult a Doctor if Symptoms Persist</strong></p><p>If you experience persistent coughing, breathing difficulties, chest pain, or irritation in eyes and throat, don't ignore it. Consult a doctor immediately. People with asthma or other respiratory conditions should keep their medications handy.</p><p><strong>✓ 10.
